Alan Turing - Father of the Modern Computer

Here are ten points that illustrates Turing's life.

  • Alan Turing was a British mathematician and logician, often considered the father of modern computer science.
  • He was born on June 23, 1912, in London, England. Turing studied at both Cambridge and Princeton universities, where he established himself as a brilliant mathematician.
  • In 1936, Turing proposed a theoretical machine, now known as the Turing machine, which became the basis for modern computers.
  • During World War II, he worked at Bletchley Park, Britain's codebreaking centre, and was instrumental in breaking the German Enigma code. His work on the Enigma code significantly aided the Allies and is thought to have shortened the war.
  • Turing was homosexual and was prosecuted for homosexuality in 1952, which was then a criminal act in Britain. He agreed to hormone therapy (chemical castration) to avoid imprisonment.
  • In 1954, Turing died from cyanide poisoning. An inquest determined his death a suicide, but some disputed this conclusion.
  • Turing's contributions to the war effort remained classified in the years following his death, but they were later acknowledged, and his pioneering work in computer science became widely recognized.
  • In 2013, Queen Elizabeth II granted Turing a posthumous pardon, and his life and work have since received significant public recognition, including in popular media.
